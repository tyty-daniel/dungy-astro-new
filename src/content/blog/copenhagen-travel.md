Seeing a big 'SALE!' notification from Ryanair pop up on my phone in August is what prompted my trip to Copenhagen. 

This is my second solo travelling trip, and my third holiday this year. My first solo trip was to Belgium, going to Brussels with a day excursion to Bruges (which is easily one of the best towns I have been to). 

Why did I choose Copenhagen? Well it was one of the cheapest for me, only buying a return ticket for £35 from Manchester, and it being frequently hailed as a safe place for solo travellers. 

## Day 1 - Sunday
I actually landed at 11pm Saturday and didn't make it to the hotel until midnight, so my first full day is the Sunday. 

I didn't leave the hotel until 10:30am, finding my way to the nearest cafe for some breakfast. Espresso House - the European equivalent to Pret-a-Manger - was decent enough for a latte and a croissant. 
The streets were littered with posters of election candidates, all having very simple, straight-forward branding. They were practically on every corner, on buses, on giant billboards, attached to fences and gates, everywhere. 

One thing about Copenhagen that totally blew my mind was the bikes. There were bikes everywhere. There were more people on bikes than there were pedestrians on the path. I felt odd for walking places rather than getting a bike, but I already had a plan for cycling the next day. 

My first stop was the Glyptotek, a private art gallery mostly consisting of Ancient Mediterranean sculptures. 

The Glypyotek's main central room was superb. A rare mosaic of Europa took centre stage of the room with grand roman sculptures of mythological figures on the outskirts, all well preserved. It felt like I was walking into a tradlarper's wet dream. 

![Glyptotek Sculptures](/glyptotek-sculptures.jpg)

The gallery also had busts of important Greek and Roman figures, such as Epicurus and Claudius, and a sizeable Egyptian and French sculpture collection. Leaving at 4pm, many of the other museums were closing soon, so I decided to have a wander around the city centre. 

To be honest, I expected everybody to be much taller, but the average height walking round wasn't that tall. The more Danish-looking people (blonde, blue eyes, Nordic face) were almost always at least 6 feet, towering over me. They were all kind and spoke English very well, which always helps when asking for help. 

There were many cool artsy design shops, to be expected from a country where specific lamps are nationally-known cultural references. 

!["Enjoy cocaine" sign](/enjoy-coke-sign.jpg)

The castles and cathedrals were all very pretty too. Seeing Fredrik's castle was certainly a highlight.

At half 7, I decided to go back to the hotel. 

## Day 2 - Monday
Today was the day of my cycling tour! I left the hotel at half 9 to get to the meeting place at around 10am. Seeing all the cyclists yesterday made me excited to finally have a go at riding around the city for real. 

The tour guide was an American who moved to Copenhagen a few years ago. I was the first one there and struck conversation with him, turned out he studied in my hometown for a few years! 

There was 17 of us in the group, which made cycling around slower than it should have been, but still an enjoyable experience overall. Very educated on the city, he taught us that Copenhagen was actually designed by the same Dutch urban planners who designed Amsterdam. This was especially apparant when seeing all the canals.

![Copenhagen canals urban planning](/dutch-planning-copenhagen.jpg)

Being shown around the self-govering Christiana and seeing all the Dutch urban planning made it worth it, though I will avoid cycling over the cobblestone roads next time -- regretted that the next day. 

After, I went through to the Amalienborg palace which I must say was very underwhelming and a waste of £15, but I know for next time.
